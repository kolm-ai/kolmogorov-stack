02 / Why the form fails

A questionnaire no longer clears it.

A filled-in form was enough when software was deterministic. An agent decides at runtime, so the reviewer wants something they can check, not read. Three things are now table stakes, and a questionnaire proves none of them.

Over-permissioned

More access than the job needs

Most agents hold ten times the privileges they use, often on one shared key. It is the first thing a reviewer flags, and a form cannot un-flag it.

ASR-1 · least privilege

No tamper-evidence

A trail nobody can trust

If the record of what your agent did can be edited after the fact, it is not evidence. Reviewers want it append-only and hash-chained, not asserted.

ASR-2 · audit trail

Say-so doesn't scale

Self-attestation shifts the burden

Every answer you write by hand becomes work the buyer's team has to take on faith or re-verify. They want proof they can run themselves.

ASR-6 · evidence

04 / Two clocks

Minutes for the scan. Days for a signature with a name on it.

A stalled review runs on two clocks, and we are candid about which one needs a person. State it the way you would to the buyer: minutes for the automated scan, days for a named-reviewed attestation.

The machine clock

No human waits here

Permission reads, audit-trail and egress checks, the prompt-injection battery, control-mapping and signing are compute. They run in minutes to hours, with nobody in the loop, and re-run on every deploy.

The human clock

A name behind the result

For the deal where a CISO wants a person to stand behind the finding, a named co-signer reviews and signs alongside the math. That is days, bounded by an SLA, against the four to eight weeks a from-scratch review takes.

The crypto proves the bytes were not altered. The name proves a person reviewed them. Most deals clear on the automated report alone.

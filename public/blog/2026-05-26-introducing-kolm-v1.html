Introducing kolm v1: Wrapper, Studio, Run and Govern.

2026-05-26 · ~8 minute read

V1 of kolm ships three surfaces that share one disk format. The Wrapper routes calls and signs receipts. Studio captures, distills, and compiles. Run and Govern deploys artifacts, monitors drift, and exports the evidence your auditors need. The whole thing is Apache-2.0 and runs on hardware you already own.

The product is one shape

Every other "AI ops" platform picks a side. Wrapper-only tools (LiteLLM, OpenRouter) get you a gateway but no path to local. Fine-tune-only tools (OpenPipe, Predibase) get you a custom model but no gateway, no receipts, no traffic to learn from. Hosted distillation services lock the artifact behind their API. Each solves a third of the problem; you glue the rest.

kolm V1 collapses the three. Use any surface independently; the value compounds when you use them together. The Wrapper sees real traffic, captures the rows that mattered, hands the dataset to Studio, and Studio hands a signed local artifact back to the Wrapper for routing. Run and Govern wraps the loop with deployment, monitoring, and audit export.

Surface 1: Wrapper

The Wrapper is a drop-in proxy in front of every LLM API your code talks to: Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, DeepSeek, Groq, Together, Fireworks, OpenRouter, and a dozen more. Point your existing client at https://kolm.ai/v1 with a kolm bearer token, keep the request shape you already write, and the call gets routed, logged, redacted, and signed without code changes.

The Wrapper does five things on every call:

  1. Tier check. Rate-limit by plan with a 10% grace buffer and standard X-RateLimit-* headers.
  2. Route selection. Per-namespace chain resolves to a provider; local artifacts first if the namespace has one.
  3. PII pass. Detect, redact, block, or off; scanned on input and output.
  4. Receipt sign. One Ed25519-signed receipt per call under kolm-audit-1, 19 canonical fields in deterministic key order.
  5. Capture write. One row in your capture lake with capture_eligible:true set on any fallback. That flag feeds the next distill.

Per-call overhead is about 40% over a direct provider call (mostly the Vercel proxy hop on the managed deployment; the next post decomposes the tax phase by phase). Cost is within 2% of direct (no token markup). When a call routes to a local artifact, cost goes to zero.

Surface 2: Studio

Studio is the workshop where captured rows turn into local artifacts. Three stages, one CLI:

kolm captures list --namespace support --status approved
kolm distill --namespace support --base qwen2.5-7b --teachers claude,gpt-4o,deepseek-r1
kolm compile --target gguf-q4km --target ollama-modelfile

Studio runs the Forge under the hood: a council distillation pipeline letting multiple teachers (Claude, GPT-4o, DeepSeek-R1, Gemini, anything you route through the Wrapper) generate rationale-bearing pairs for one student, weighted by your cost budget. Trinity-500 (the first published kolm artifact) was distilled this way: 410 pairs in 79 seconds of LoRA on a 5090.

Compile exports to GGUF (Q2 through Q8 plus IQ family), Ollama Modelfile, EXL2, GPTQ, AWQ, FP8, NVFP4, HQQ, and MLX. Each export carries a runtime passport: target, precision, memory, quality delta from FP16.

Surface 3: Run and Govern

An artifact you cannot deploy is a research project. Run and Govern turns the compiled file into a running service and gives you the evidence you need to keep running it.

Run side:

  • kolm serve artifact.kolm auto-detects format and hardware, picks the right runtime (vLLM, llama.cpp, MLX, Ollama, transformers), exposes OpenAI-compatible /v1/chat/completions.
  • Generators for Docker Compose, Kubernetes manifests (Deployment, Service, HPA, ConfigMap, PVC, init), vLLM configs, and an air-gap bundle.

Govern side:

  • Artifact lifecycle. created, signed, deployed, monitored, superseded, revoked, archived; every transition recorded with timestamp, actor, reason, evidence id.
  • Evidence DAG. Captures, evals, teachers, students, runtimes, signatures, policies, rights as nodes; derived-from, validated-by, invalidates, supersedes as edges. Revoke flips dependents to needs_review.
  • Assurance case export. JSON or PDF mapping the artifact to SOC 2, HIPAA, NIST AI RMF, or EU AI Act controls with evidence ids attached.
  • Drift detection. Embedding similarity and KL divergence prod vs training, fallback-rate trend, alerting. On fire, kolm recommends a re-distill with capture priorities pre-set.
  • Cost displacement reporting. Per-namespace frontier baseline vs actual, monthly savings, cumulative, payback.

The confidence router is the glue

The piece that ties Wrapper to Studio is the gateway confidence router. On every call, it runs your local artifact first, measures the response confidence (logit entropy if the engine exposes log-probs, a self-judge prompt if not, or a hedging-keyword scan as last resort), and falls back to the frontier provider in the chain only when confidence drops below a per-namespace threshold (default 0.55).

Every fallback is automatically tagged capture_eligible:true. That row is the next training example. After a few iterations the local artifact serves the long tail of your traffic at roughly $0 per call and about 1.2 seconds, and frontier handles the genuinely-novel queries. The loop is documented in detail at /docs/gateway-confidence-router.

What makes V1 different

  • One disk format. The .kolm file carries the weights, the runtime passport, the evidence DAG, the K-Score evaluation block, and the Ed25519 signature in one tarball. Same file works on a Mac for prototyping, a server for production, and an air-gapped device for compliance.
  • Receipts are mandatory. Every Wrapper call gets one, success or failure. You cannot opt out of the audit trail; you can verify it for free at /v1/verify/<receipt_id> with no auth.
  • Apache-2.0. The whole stack (Wrapper, Studio, runtime adapters, SDKs in Node, Python, Rust, C, and MCP) is on GitHub at kolm-ai/kolm. Self-host with one command.
  • No managed-only features. Everything on the managed kolm.ai also runs in your BYOC (bring your own cluster) on Modal, RunPod, Colab, or a 5090 under your desk.

How to start

Three options, in increasing order of commitment:

# 1. One-call sanity check (no signup, 20 free calls per day per IP)
curl https://kolm.ai/v1/free/chat \
  -d '{"messages":[{"role":"user","content":"hello"}]}'

# 2. Sign up, get a key, point your existing OpenAI client
export OPENAI_BASE_URL=https://kolm.ai/v1
export OPENAI_API_KEY=ks_...

# 3. Distill your first local artifact from your real traffic
kolm captures list --namespace support
kolm distill --namespace support --base qwen2.5-7b
kolm serve artifact.kolm

The Wrapper, Studio, and Run and Govern docs each have a top-level overview at /wrapper, /studio, and /docs/run/overview. The Trinity-500 case study (a 7B that beats Claude Haiku and ties gpt-4o-mini on the customer-support rubric at half the response length) is up next on the blog.

Caveats. The 40% wrapper-overhead figure is from our managed deployment that proxies provider keys through Vercel; a Railway-direct deployment with the provider key on the same host cuts that to roughly 10 to 50 milliseconds. Trinity-500 is one customer-support distillation on Qwen2.5-7B; your numbers will vary by domain, base model, and council composition. The whole product is V1, not 1.0 perfect; we will write about the rough edges as we find them.

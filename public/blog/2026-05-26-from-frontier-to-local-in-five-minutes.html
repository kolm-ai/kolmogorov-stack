From frontier to local in five minutes.

2026-05-26 · ~10 minute read

The whole kolm thesis fits in one sentence: route through the gateway, capture the calls the frontier had to handle, distill a local artifact from those captures, deploy it, watch local handle most of the traffic next month. Here is the worked example with real commands. Five minutes once the CLI is installed; the GPU does the work in the background.

The flywheel in one diagram

  app  ->  kolm gateway  ->  [local artifact (if confidence ok)]
                          ->  [frontier provider (on fallback)] -> capture
                                                                  |
                                                                  v
                                                              kolm distill
                                                                  |
                                                                  v
                                                          local artifact v2
                                                                  |
                                                                  v
                                                          deploy on top of v1

Every loop around that diagram lifts the local share of your traffic and lowers the per-call cost. The first loop is the longest because you have nothing local yet; later loops compress to "kolm distill, kolm serve, done."

Minute 1

Install and point your client

kolm ships as a single Node CLI plus six SDKs. Install in one command:

npm i -g github:sneaky-hippo/kolm-stack
kolm login    # opens browser, returns ks_... bearer
kolm whoami   # confirms key is live, prints tenant

If you already use the OpenAI or Anthropic SDK, swap the base URL. Code does not change:

# Python (works for openai, anthropic, langchain, llamaindex, ...)
export OPENAI_BASE_URL=https://kolm.ai/v1
export OPENAI_API_KEY=ks_xxx
# Every call your app makes now flows through the gateway.

Anthropic users get ANTHROPIC_BASE_URL + ANTHROPIC_API_KEY. The gateway preserves whichever SDK shape you started with; no translation layer.

Minute 2

Run real traffic, get receipts

One call to prove the loop works:

curl https://kolm.ai/v1/chat/completions \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer $OPENAI_API_KEY" \
  -d '{
    "model": "claude-haiku-4-5",
    "messages": [{"role":"user","content":"What is your refund policy?"}]
  }'

The response comes back in OpenAI shape (because OpenAI was the client SDK), wrapping an Anthropic call (because the model id resolved through the namespace chain). Buried in the response envelope is a kolm_receipt_id field. Pull the receipt:

curl https://kolm.ai/v1/verify/rcpt_01KYC...
# returns the full signed receipt + verification result
# no auth required, can be shared with anyone

Every call you make from here on emits one receipt. After a week of real traffic you have a few thousand receipts and a few thousand capture rows.

Minute 3

Inspect the captures

The capture lake holds the prompt, response, route decision, confidence, and capture-eligible flag for every gateway call. Filter to the ones that are interesting:

kolm captures list \
  --namespace support \
  --status pending \
  --fallback-only \
  --last 7d

--fallback-only returns only rows marked capture_eligible:true (the local artifact was not confident enough and the gateway fell back). These are the high-value training rows: the model handled them, you have the ground truth (the frontier response), and they are exactly where the next local model needs to improve.

Review one, then approve in bulk:

kolm captures show cap_01KYC...
kolm captures approve --bulk --namespace support \
  --status pending --fallback-only --last 7d \
  --filter "confidence_below=0.55"

Or click through interactively at /account/captures/review. Approval is human-gated by design; an AI-approved capture is a credibility risk.

Minute 4

Distill a local artifact

Once you have a few hundred approved captures, the distill is one command. kolm picks reasonable defaults; you can override every one of them:

kolm distill \
  --namespace support \
  --base qwen2.5-7b-instruct \
  --teachers claude-opus-4-7,gpt-4o,deepseek-r1-distill-qwen-32b \
  --teacher-weights 0.6,0.3,0.1 \
  --since-last-distill \
  --output ./support-v1.kolm

What that does, end to end:

  1. Pulls every approved capture from the support namespace since the last distill ran (the first time, "since" is "ever").
  2. For each capture, runs the three teachers in parallel (through the kolm proxy, no need for direct provider keys on your machine) to generate a council response, weighted by the teacher weights you set.
  3. Trains a LoRA adapter on the merged council pairs on whichever GPU the kolm CLI sees, with the fast bf16 plus grad-checkpoint plus batch=1 recipe that fits a 7B in any modern card.
  4. Merges the adapter into a full-weight checkpoint, signs the resulting bytes with your Ed25519 signing key, writes a passport (provenance, hyperparameters, K-Score evaluation block, runtime passport per target), wraps the whole thing in a tarball, and gives you support-v1.kolm.

On a 5090 with a few hundred pairs that is about 90 seconds. On a laptop, on a smaller card, or in Modal or Colab if you do not have a local GPU, it takes longer; the same command works on any of them. (See the Trinity-500 case study earlier on this blog for what 410 pairs and 79 seconds on a 5090 look like in detail.)

Minute 5

Deploy and watch the loop close

Two options. If you want the artifact behind the kolm gateway so the confidence router takes over:

kolm namespace deploy support --artifact ./support-v1.kolm
kolm namespace config support \
  --chain "local:support-v1,anthropic:claude-haiku-4-5" \
  --confidence-threshold 0.60 \
  --capture-fallbacks

From here every call to the support namespace tries the local artifact first. If the local response has confidence >= 0.60 (computed from logit entropy if the runtime exposes log-probs, a self-judge prompt if not, or a hedging-keyword scan as last resort), that response is returned. Otherwise the call falls back to claude-haiku-4-5 and the capture row gets tagged capture_eligible:true for the next distill.

If you would rather serve the artifact yourself (on-prem, air-gapped, or to keep the gateway out of the path entirely):

# Auto-detect format + hardware + runtime
kolm serve ./support-v1.kolm --port 8080

# Or force a runtime
kolm serve ./support-v1.kolm --runtime vllm --port 8080
kolm serve ./support-v1.kolm --runtime llama-cpp --gpu-layers 35
kolm serve ./support-v1.kolm --runtime ollama
kolm serve ./support-v1.kolm --runtime mlx           # Apple Silicon

Either way the server exposes /v1/chat/completions in OpenAI shape, so your existing client does not know whether it is hitting frontier or local. Swap the URL, keep your code.

What changes over the next month

Day 1 your namespace is at 0% local share (no local artifact yet). Day 7, after the first distill, the share is typically 60 to 75% on a narrow namespace; the easy 60% of cases are well-covered by a single distill, the long tail is the harder grind. Each subsequent distill (we recommend weekly until your drift detector tells you to slow down) compresses the long tail further:

IterationPairs trained onLocal shareCost per 1k calls
v0 (no local)00%$0.30
v1 (week 1)~400~70%~$0.09
v2 (week 2)~800~85%~$0.05
v3 (week 3)~1200~92%~$0.024
v4 (week 4)~1600~96%~$0.012

Numbers depend on the namespace; a narrow domain with low entropy (support, classification, structured extraction) compresses faster than an open domain (creative writing, code generation across many languages). The two signals to watch are local-share-percent (rising) and fallback-rate-on-novel-queries (steady or falling). When either trend reverses, the drift detector fires; that is your signal to look at whether the workload has shifted under you.

The receipts make the loop credible

One thing worth pulling out: every step of this flywheel is receipt-backed. Every gateway call has a signed receipt. Every capture row points to its receipt. Every distill carries a passport whose evidence DAG names the captures it trained on. Every deploy records a lifecycle event with the artifact id and the actor. If you want to know "did this specific deployed answer come from the local model or the frontier, on which version, trained on which captures, signed by which key," it is a chain of references with no gap.

The cost displacement reporting puts a number on it:

kolm savings --namespace support --period last-30d
# 1,247,389 calls
# frontier baseline: $375.42
# actual spend: $14.91
# savings: $360.51 (96%)
# payback period: 0.2 months (compile was $87 on Modal)

That is the version of the story you take to whoever is paying the bill. The receipts are the version you take to the auditor.

Constraints. Five minutes is the wall-clock for the CLI commands, not for the GPU work; the LoRA itself takes between 80 seconds (Trinity-500 on a 5090) and 20 minutes (CPU-only on a laptop) depending on hardware. The local-share table is a typical pattern, not a guarantee; we have seen namespaces hit 95% by week 1 and others stall at 60% for weeks because the long tail is genuinely novel. The "deploy on top of v1" step assumes the new artifact passes your eval; kolm holds the old artifact deployed until you sign off on the swap.

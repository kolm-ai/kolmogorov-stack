kolm vs OpenAI fine-tune

OpenAI fine-tuning is the shortest path when the OpenAI API is your only deploy target and residency is not a constraint. kolm is for teams that need the tuned behavior as a signed file they can own, audit, and run elsewhere.

Use OpenAI when hosted custom models are enough. Use kolm when the behavior needs to become a portable signed artifact that runs on your hardware, edge devices, or an air-gapped VPC.

Where they actually differ.

The matrix above is the executive summary. The five sections below are the substance: why a buyer with a regulated workload, repetitive prompts, or an edge requirement ends up at kolm, and why a buyer with hosted-only constraints and no residency concern ends up at OpenAI.

Cost per inference at scale.

OpenAI's fine-tune SKU prices inference at a markup over the base model. A tuned GPT-4.1 mini lands around $3 per 1M input tokens and $12 per 1M output tokens in 2026 pricing. That is excellent for a few thousand requests per day. At a few million requests per day, the same workload running on a 7B INT4 student on a 3090 amortizes to roughly $0.30 per 1M tokens once you account for power and depreciation. The crossover happens around 50k to 200k requests per day depending on prompt length and output length. Below that crossover, OpenAI is cheaper than running your own GPU; above it, kolm is cheaper by an order of magnitude. The decision is workload volume and the duty cycle of the hardware you would buy.

Data exfiltration boundary.

OpenAI's standard tier logs request and response data for safety review for 30 days; the Enterprise tier with ZDR (zero data retention) plus the Healthcare BAA pulls that boundary in. Even with ZDR, the data crossed OpenAI's network and was processed in their infrastructure. For tenants whose regulator or auditor reads the contract literally, that is a different posture from "the inference happened inside our VPC and the bytes never left." kolm's compile step calls a teacher (which may be OpenAI), but the teacher is called only on the tenant's seed corpus and only with the PHI redactor applied; the resulting student runs locally and never calls out again. The exfiltration surface shrinks from "every production request" to "a one-time, redacted compile traffic" and that is what your auditor will read.

Frozen-eval semantics.

OpenAI's fine-tune console gives you an eval UX: dashboards, metrics, the ability to compare runs. There is no compile-time block that prevents a regressed tune from being served. The eval is informational. kolm makes the eval the gate: K-score V2 with A/T/R/F/E/Z axes is computed at compile, the result is signed into the artifact's receipt chain, and the ship verb refuses to emit the.kolm file if the score is below the threshold the spec declared. The artifact you carry off the build machine is one that passed. That semantic is what compliance teams call a frozen eval, and it is structurally different from a dashboard.

Cancellation surface (your model on your hardware).

If OpenAI deprecates the base model your fine-tune was built on, your tuned model goes with it. If OpenAI raises the fine-tune token price, your unit economics change overnight. If OpenAI terminates your account for any reason, your tuned model is gone. None of these are theoretical: every major API vendor has done at least two of them in the last 24 months. A .kolm file on your disk is not subject to any of these. The vendor relationship at the teacher step is real and ongoing, but once the artifact is signed and sitting on your filesystem it runs without kolm.ai or OpenAI existing. That is the cancellation surface buyers care about for production loads they cannot let blink.

What you get back.

OpenAI gives you a custom model ID. That is a sufficient artifact for a hosted-only deploy and a deep convenience for teams that have zero ML infrastructure. kolm gives you a signed file with a 14-step receipt chain (spec, seeds, split, train, recipes, evals, external holdout, tenant shadow, auditor, supersession, drift, sigs, rekor), an Ed25519 signature, a Sigstore entry, and a manifest that names every input. The file is what your auditor reads; the receipt chain is what the auditor verifies; the .kolm extension is what your runtime consumes. Two different artifacts for two different deployment shapes.

What to use kolm for.

  • Regulated data. HIPAA PHI, KYC, privileged legal traffic, classified or export-controlled workflows. The PHI redactor plus the local inference boundary plus the cryptographic receipt chain is the artifact your compliance team can attest to.
  • Edge or on-prem deploy. Air-gapped VPC, ship-borne, factory-floor, retail-store, or phone deploy. The .kolm file ships through any pipe that ships a binary; no runtime network is required.
  • Repetitive prompts that justify distillation. If 60% of your traffic asks the same shape of question (form normalization, denial-code classification, retrieval reranking, schema-aware extraction), the distilled student matches the teacher on that distribution at a fraction of the cost per call.
  • Auditor-readable provenance. A regulator or buyer who asks "what teacher trained this, on what data, with what eval, signed by whom" gets a single signed file plus a receipt chain instead of a console screenshot.
  • Cost ceiling at high volume. Once you cross roughly 50k to 200k requests per day on a stable task, your own GPU plus a kolm artifact is cheaper than any token-based tuned-model SKU and the savings compound monthly.

What to use OpenAI fine-tune for.

  • Hosted-only deploy with no residency constraint. Your production target is the OpenAI API and your buyers do not ask where the inference happened. The fine-tune SKU is the cleanest path from JSONL to working tuned model. Skip the rest.
  • Zero ML operations capacity. You have no GPU, no MLOps person, no appetite to operate a model in production. The managed UX from upload to deploy is what you are paying for and it is genuinely good.
  • Low or moderate request volume. Below the cost crossover (roughly 50k requests per day), token-based pricing on a managed tune is cheaper than the all-in cost of owning a GPU plus the labor to keep it running.
  • Frontier-base behaviors that do not distill cleanly. If your task requires the full GPT-4.1 or o-series capability and a 7B student plateaus below your quality bar, the right answer is the frontier-base tune, not a forced distillation.

The layered pattern (when you use both).

The plain middle of the market is teams that use OpenAI as the teacher inside a kolm compile. You keep the frontier-grade capability where it earns its cost (the few percent of requests that genuinely need it) and you ship a kolm artifact for the 90%+ of traffic that is repetitive. OpenAI's API becomes the teacher SKU on the build side; kolm's signed file becomes the runtime artifact on the serve side. The bill drops by an order of magnitude and the auditor reads a receipt chain instead of a console.

Licensing note. OpenAI's API terms restrict using outputs to develop "competing" general-purpose models. A narrow-task kolm artifact (form normalizer, denial-code classifier, single-vertical extractor) is not a competing general model; the legal status of the narrow-task carve-out is unsettled. The always-safe option is --teacher=local:<open-weight-model> on your own GPU. See RS-1 §7.7 for the posture.

Synthetic data

Synthetic data is training material generated by a model rather than collected from humans. It is cheap, scalable, and the dominant fuel for modern distillation pipelines. Without diversity control, real-data anchoring, and provenance tracking it accumulates mode-collapse failures (sometimes called model autophagy) within a few generations.

What it is

The lineage from Self-Instruct (Wang et al. 2022) through Alpaca (Taori et al. 2023) and WizardLM (Xu et al. 2023) to the Phi series (Gunasekar et al. 2023, Textbooks Are All You Need) established that a strong teacher prompted with a structured generator can manufacture a training corpus that beats human-labelled data on the metrics it was generated for. The Phi-1 result, 350M parameters at 1.3B-parameter capability on coding, hinged entirely on synthetic textbook-quality data. The cost is two failure modes the literature now treats as established. First, mode collapse: Shumailov et al. (2024, AI Models Collapse When Trained on Recursively Generated Data, Nature) showed that training a model on its own outputs across generations causes the tails of the distribution to vanish and the centre to narrow. Second, calibration drift: a student trained only on its teacher's high-confidence answers loses the ability to express uncertainty. The counter-measures the field has converged on are real-data anchoring (mix in a fraction of human-labelled rows), diversity scoring (drop low-entropy or near-duplicate synthetic rows), and full provenance tracking so a buyer can audit what was generated by which model under which prompt.

How kolm uses it

Most distillation corpora in kolm v0.2.6 are synthetic: prompts are mined from the capture log, responses are sampled from the teacher, and the pair is the training row. The pipeline runs three diversity filters by default. A near-duplicate filter uses MinHash on 5-grams with a Jaccard threshold of 0.9 to drop redundant rows (see /research/holdout for the spillover-prevention version of the same filter). A semantic diversity filter requires that no more than 30 percent of rows fall within any single embedding cluster (k-means, k chosen by silhouette). A response-entropy filter drops rows where the teacher emitted a near-identical response across paraphrased prompts, which is the signature of teacher mode collapse. Real-data anchoring is recipe-controlled: when the buyer marks any capture-log rows as ground_truth:true, the trainer up-weights them by a configurable factor (default 3x). Every synthetic row's provenance (teacher id, prompt template hash, sampling temperature, capture timestamp) is recorded in the SBOM (/research/sbom) and the receipt chain.

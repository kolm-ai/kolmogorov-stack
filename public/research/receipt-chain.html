Receipt chain

A receipt chain binds the inputs of a pipeline to its outputs with canonical hashes and signatures. Tenant-local integrity can use HMAC when both sides share a secret; public artifact distribution adds Ed25519 and optional transparency-log anchoring so third parties can verify without receiving the tenant secret.

What it is

The construction is a stripped-down version of the hash-chain primitive used in transparency logs (Crosby and Wallach, 2009; the Certificate Transparency project, RFC 6962; Trillian, the open-source transparency-log infrastructure). Where a Merkle tree summarizes a set of leaves, a hash chain summarizes a sequence in causal order. The HMAC variant adds a long-lived shared secret so a chain root is only meaningful if the verifier holds the right key. This lets a service expose a chain root publicly without leaking the per-link content; the chain proves only "the holder of this key ordered these tags in this sequence". For ML supply-chain use, the chain links cover the steps a buyer cares about: the captured prompt, the derived training row, the executed training step, and the signed artifact. Each link references the hash of the previous link's tag.

How kolm uses it

The kolm runtime emits a four-ring HMAC receipt chain for every signed artifact. Ring 1 (pre-image) is the HMAC of the captured prompt and response together with the recipe id. Ring 2 (derived) is the HMAC of ring 1 with the corpus row hash (the post-transform, post-redaction training example). Ring 3 (execution) is the HMAC of ring 2 with the trainer config hash, the base-model hash, and the post-train checkpoint hash. Ring 4 (seal) is the HMAC of ring 3 with the K-score gate result, the holdout hash, and the artifact's content-address. The chain root (ring 4) is published in the receipt and signed by the platform key. Verification is offline: anyone with the receipt, the artifact, the chain key (per-tenant), and the inputs can recompute each ring and compare. The CLI kolm verify <artifact> walks the chain locally. The chain key is rotated on a schedule and old roots remain verifiable via the published rotation log.

References

Laurie, B., Langley, A., Kasper, E. Certificate Transparency. RFC 6962, 2013.

Krawczyk, H., Bellare, M., Canetti, R. HMAC: Keyed-Hashing for Message Authentication. RFC 2104, 1997.

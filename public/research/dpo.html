Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)

DPO reformulates the standard RLHF pipeline (reward model + PPO) as a single classification loss on pairs of preferred and rejected responses. No reward network. No online sampling. The closed-form loss makes the implicit reward of any language model the log-ratio of its probabilities under the trained and reference policies.

What it is

Rafailov et al. (2023) showed that under the Bradley-Terry preference model, the optimal RLHF policy has a closed-form relationship with the reward function and a fixed KL-regularized reference policy. Inverting that relationship gives a loss that depends only on the log-probabilities of paired chosen and rejected responses under the policy and the reference. The loss is -log sigmoid(beta * (log pi(yw|x) / pi_ref(yw|x) - log pi(yl|x) / pi_ref(yl|x))), where yw is the preferred response, yl the rejected, and beta controls how aggressively the policy departs from the reference. The training loop is plain supervised: two forward passes per pair (policy and frozen reference), no rollouts, no on-policy sampling, no separate reward model. This makes DPO cheap to run, stable on small data, and easy to integrate into existing fine-tuning infrastructure.

How kolm uses it

In kolm v0.2.6 the preference path uses SFT on response-distillation outputs (/research/response-distill). DPO ships in v0.3 as the default preference-optimization stage when the buyer's spec includes paired-comparison labels (from the holdout judges or from human-labeled marketplace examples). The implementation follows the TRL reference; the receipt block records beta, the reference checkpoint hash, the pair count, and the implicit-reward margin distribution on the holdout. Tasks without paired preferences will continue to use SFT plus distillation as the only loss.

References

Rafailov, R., Sharma, A., Mitchell, E., Ermon, S., Manning, C. D., Finn, C. Direct Preference Optimization: Your Language Model is Secretly a Reward Model. NeurIPS 2023. arXiv:2305.18290.

Bradley, R. A., Terry, M. E. Rank Analysis of Incomplete Block Designs. Biometrika, 1952.

Ouyang, L. et al. Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback. NeurIPS 2022. arXiv:2203.02155.

von Werra, L. et al. TRL: Transformer Reinforcement Learning. Hugging Face, 2024.

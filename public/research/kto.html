Kahneman-Tversky Optimization (KTO)

KTO trains a policy on single labelled responses (this one is desirable, that one is not) rather than on pairwise comparisons. The loss is a prospect-theoretic value function: gains are weighted by a concave utility, losses by a steeper convex one, with the implicit reward defined the same way as in DPO. The result is a preference-optimization recipe that works on the kind of labels real-world ops teams actually collect.

What it is

Ethayarajh et al. (2024) start from the observation that human preference data is almost never collected as clean pairs. Operators flag bad customer-service replies, ML platforms log thumbs-up on individual generations, RLHF teams keep large piles of single examples annotated with a binary label. DPO and PPO both need pairs. KTO sidesteps the requirement by deriving a loss from Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory: the implicit reward is the log-likelihood ratio between the policy and a reference (same as DPO), but the loss applies a kinked utility function around a reference point. Desirable examples push the reward above the reference; undesirable examples push it below; the asymmetry of the utility encodes loss-aversion. Empirically the paper shows KTO matches or slightly outperforms DPO on standard benchmarks even when paired data is available, and is the only viable option when only unpaired labels exist.

How kolm uses it

KTO is the preferred research-track preference optimizer for the kind of feedback the capture layer naturally collects. A tenant's captured prompts often include thumbs-up/thumbs-down signals from dev-agent telemetry, accept-or-reject heuristics on code completions, and sentiment tags on customer-service drafts more often than clean preference pairs. The KTO track takes a captured-rows manifest with per-row binary labels and runs KTO with the standard beta=0.1, lambda_D=1.0 for desirable and lambda_U=1.0 for undesirable defaults from the paper. The receipt records beta, lambda values, desirable-to-undesirable ratio, and post-KTO holdout K-score.

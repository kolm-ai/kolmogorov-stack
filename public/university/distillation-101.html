Distillation 101.

Frontier AI on your own infrastructure starts here. This lesson explains what knowledge distillation is, why you would do it, how the kolm pipeline works, and walks through one fully worked example so you can see the real numbers.

Not Kolm therapeutics. This lesson is part of kolm.ai, the open-source AI workbench.

Level: Introductory Read time: ~15 min Prereq: Comfort with a terminal Hands-on: Optional

What is knowledge distillation

Knowledge distillation is the practice of training a small student model to imitate the behavior of a large teacher model on a specific task. The teacher is something expensive and general — Claude, GPT-4, a 70B open-weight model running on rented H100s. The student is something cheap and narrow — a 1B to 13B open-weight model that you can run on your own GPU or even your laptop.

The intuition is older than it looks. In a 2015 paper Geoffrey Hinton, Oriol Vinyals, and Jeff Dean showed that the probability distribution a large model puts over its outputs — not just the single most-likely answer — contains a lot of structure. A small model trained to match that distribution learns more than it would from the hard labels alone. The teacher tells the student not only "the answer is A" but also "B is implausible, C is similar to A, D is wildly wrong." That richer signal lets a small student match a large teacher's quality on a narrow slice of behavior, often within a few percentage points.

Modern open-weight teachers (Llama, Qwen, DeepSeek) and frontier API teachers (Claude, GPT, Gemini) have made distillation practical for almost any task you can describe in prompts. If you can write a prompt and get a good answer from the big model, you can probably distill that capability into a small model you control.

Why distill?

People come to distillation for four different reasons, usually mixed.

  • Cost. A frontier API call costs cents. The same query through a distilled 7B model running on hardware you already own costs essentially zero per call. At any meaningful traffic volume, the math is brutal — ten million calls a month at one cent each is a six-figure annual bill, and most of those calls are doing the same kind of work that a specialist could handle.
  • Latency. A round trip to a hosted model is bounded by network and queueing. A model in your own datacenter or on the user's device responds in single-digit milliseconds. For interactive UI, autocomplete, voice, and agentic loops, that difference is the difference between feeling instant and feeling sluggish.
  • Control. You decide when the model changes. Hosted APIs deprecate, silently re-tune, change refusal behavior, and add price hikes. A distilled artifact on your hardware does not change unless you change it. For regulated workloads — healthcare triage, legal extraction, financial review — that is the only acceptable posture.
  • On-prem and data residency. Some data cannot leave your network. PHI, FERPA-covered student records, attorney-client communications, EU-resident personal data under the AI Act. A distilled artifact running on hardware you physically control sidesteps an entire category of compliance work.

You should not distill when the task is too broad (a chatbot that answers anything), when the volume is too low (a hundred calls a month is not worth the engineering), or when you have no clean way to evaluate "did the student match the teacher" (no held-out set, no domain expert to grade outputs).

How kolm does it

The kolm pipeline has four stages. Every stage emits a signed receipt, and the final artifact bundles all the receipts together so the whole chain is auditable.

  1. Capture. You point kolm at your existing model traffic — the OpenAI or Anthropic SDK call, the LangChain hop, the agent loop — and it transparently logs the prompt, the response, and the metadata. No code changes beyond a base URL swap. Captures land in your namespace, tenant-fenced, with PII redaction available as a worker.
  2. Bake-off. Before you train anything, you replay the captures against a candidate set of student models and score them against the teacher's responses. The bake-off harness ranks candidates by K-score so you pick the smallest model that still meets your quality bar.
  3. Compile. You write a short spec describing the task, the seed captures to use, the held-out captures to evaluate against, and the deployment target. kolm compile distills the student, runs the evaluations, and packages everything into a single .kolm artifact.
  4. Deploy. The artifact is a self-contained zip with the weights, the recipe, the K-score report, the receipt chain, and the runtime metadata. You ship it to your own server, to an edge device, to a phone via CoreML or ONNX Mobile, or to a kolm-hosted gateway.

The quickstart at /docs/quickstart walks the whole pipeline in under five minutes against a toy task. The cookbook at /docs covers a dozen real ones.

Why a signed artifact? The receipt chain matters because the artifact is the only contract between you and the model. If a regulator, an auditor, or your CISO asks "what data trained this thing and how do you know it works," you hand them the .kolm and they can verify the answer themselves. No SaaS dashboard, no vendor portal, no trust-us. The format spec is at /spec/rs-1.

Worked example: Claude 4 -> Qwen 7B on a coding task

To make all of this concrete, here is one fully worked example. The task is GitHub-style code review — an engineer pushes a diff, the model produces a structured review with severity, file, line, and a suggested fix. We will distill Anthropic's Claude 4 down into Qwen 2.5 7B running on a single RTX 5090.

StageDetailHonest number
CapturesReal review traffic, six weeks5,000 prompt-response pairs
Train / held-outSplit, with hidden fold4,000 train / 750 val / 250 hidden
TeacherAnthropic Claude 4API, captured via wrapper
StudentQwen 2.5 7B InstructOpen weights, quantized NF4 + double
HardwareOne RTX 5090 (32 GB)Local workstation, no cloud
Wall timeEnd-to-end, capture to artifact~3 hours
K-scoreCombined accuracy + calibration + tail~0.87 vs teacher 0.92
Artifact sizeQuantized, single .kolm zip~5.2 GB
Runtime costPer million reviews, your hardware~ $4 in electricity

What that K-score gap means in practice: on the hidden fold, the student matches the teacher's severity rating about 91% of the time, identifies the same file-and-line about 94% of the time, and produces a suggested fix a senior reviewer rates "equivalent or better" about 80% of the time (versus 86% for the teacher). For most teams that is well inside the bar where the cost and latency wins are worth the small quality drop. For some teams it is not, and the right call is a bigger student, more captures, or a different split between local and API.

Numbers above are an illustrative reference run, not a benchmark. Your captures, your task, your hardware, and your evaluation rubric will produce different numbers. The point of the example is to show the shape of a real distillation run: thousands of captures, hours of GPU time, a K-score in the high eighties, an artifact you can hold in one hand. We publish reproducible numbers per model size at /benchmarkscompare.

Open-source AI workbench

Distill a coding assistant.

Take a frontier coding model, capture how your team actually uses it across your real repos, then distill that behavior onto a 7B open-weight model that runs on one GPU. The result is a local assistant tuned to your stack, your idioms, and your reviewer style, at a fraction of the API cost. This recipe takes a representative day-of-runtime and walks every CLI command.

What you will build

A 7B INT4 coding assistant that answers Python questions for your team and produces patches in your house style. The example target is a 30x cost reduction versus calling a frontier API on every diff. In a representative run we have seen roughly $0.0001 per call on a self-hosted RTX 5090 versus roughly $0.003 per call on a hosted frontier API. Your numbers will differ — treat the figures here as a worked example, not a guarantee.

The artifact ships as a signed .kolm file with an attached K-Score, eval coverage report, and the attestation block describing the compile environment. You can run it under vLLM, SGLang, or TGI without changing any code on the caller side.

What you need

  • kolm CLI — install from source with npm i -g @kolmogorov/cli or pip install git+https://github.com/sneaky-hippo/kolmogorov-stack@main#subdirectory=sdk/python.
  • API key — kolm login, or sign up at /signup for a free key. Keys live in ~/.kolm/auth.json.
  • Capture source — an existing OpenAI-compatible client your team already uses (Cursor, Continue, an in-house wrapper, etc.). Captures land in a namespaced local lake.
  • GPU for compile — an RTX 4090, RTX 5090, A100, or H100 is enough for a 7B INT4 student. You can also offload compile to a managed runner with kolm compile --remote.
  • Eval corpus — about 500 to 5000 captured request/response pairs labeled by your team for thumbs-up or thumbs-down.

Step-by-step

Step 1

Capture real traffic

Point your existing OpenAI client at the local proxy. Captures flow into the coding-assistant namespace and never leave your machine.

export OPENAI_BASE_URL=http://localhost:8787/v1
export KOLM_NAMESPACE=coding-assistant

kolm capture log --namespace coding-assistant --tail

Let this run for one to two weeks while your team works normally. The recipe needs roughly 5000 high-signal captures to produce a stable student.

Step 2

Bake-off candidate students

Score Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Lite, and Llama-3.1-8B against a 500-capture holdout before you commit to a long compile.

kolm bake-off \
  --namespace coding-assistant \
  --candidates qwen2.5-coder-7b,deepseek-coder-v2-lite,llama-3.1-8b \
  --holdout 500 \
  --judge teacher

The bake-off prints a leaderboard with K-Score and tokens/sec per candidate. Pick the winner.

Step 3

Compile to INT4

Distill onto the winning student and quantize to INT4 in one pass. The artifact is signed and version-pinned.

kolm compile \
  --namespace coding-assistant \
  --target qwen2.5-coder-7b-int4 \
  --epochs 3 \
  --max-seq-len 8192 \
  --output ./artifacts/coding-assistant-v1.kolm

Expect roughly three hours on an RTX 5090 for a 5000-capture corpus. Compile is resumable — kolm compile --resume picks up the last checkpoint.

Step 4

Verify the artifact

Print the signed manifest. Confirm the K-Score, eval coverage, and attestation block all read as expected.

kolm verify ./artifacts/coding-assistant-v1.kolm --pretty

If K-Score is below your ship-gate (we use 0.80 in this example), iterate the corpus before shipping. K-Score methodology explains the score.

Step 5

Deploy

Ship to your runtime of choice. The artifact is portable across vLLM, SGLang, TGI, and TensorRT-LLM.

kolm deploy ./artifacts/coding-assistant-v1.kolm \
  --runtime vllm \
  --endpoint https://gpu.internal:8000

# point team clients at the new endpoint
export OPENAI_BASE_URL=https://gpu.internal:8000/v1

Roll out behind a feature flag for the first week so you can flip back to the teacher if drift shows up.

Real numbers (example run)

From a representative 5000-capture compile on a single RTX 5090. These are example numbers from one team's run, not a generic benchmark.

  • Compile time — example: ~3 hours for 5000 captures, 3 epochs, max-seq-len 8192.
  • K-Score — example: 0.87 against the teacher on a 500-capture holdout, with the team's thumbs-down labels as ground truth.
  • Cost reduction — example: ~30x lower per-call cost compared to the frontier API on the same workload, calculated against amortized GPU rent.
  • p95 latency — example: ~210ms first token on an RTX 5090 versus ~900ms first token on the hosted API.
  • Footprint — example: 5.2 GB VRAM at INT4 versus 14.2 GB at BF16. Fits alongside other workloads on a single 24 GB card.

All numbers above are example values from one representative run. Treat them as a worked example, not a guarantee. Your corpus and your hardware will land somewhere else.

Common pitfalls

  • Low capture diversity. If 80% of your captures are auto-complete on the same file, the student over-fits to one shape and falls over on review-style requests. Filter for diversity before compile with kolm capture filter --min-distinct-files 200.
  • Misreading K-Score. K-Score is a relative score against the teacher on your corpus. A 0.87 means the student matches teacher behavior on 87% of your holdout — it does not mean the assistant is 87% correct in absolute terms. Always pair K-Score with at least one task-level eval.
  • Drift after a teacher update. When the frontier teacher ships a new version, the student starts to drift. The wrapper logs the teacher hash on every capture; re-compile when the rolling K-Score on fresh traffic drops more than 5 points.

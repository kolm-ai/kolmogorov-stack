02 / Audit, Sign, Verify

One pipeline. Three moves.

Audit the agent, sign the findings, hand your buyer a report they verify themselves. Each step is reproducible, scoped to what was tested, and checkable by anyone you give it to.

01 · Audit We examine the agent

Onramp through a log import or a sidecar proxy. Permissions, audit trail, data egress and injection resistance, each mapped to the standards your buyer cites.

02 · Sign We seal the findings

The report is canonicalized and signed with Ed25519. The signature covers the exact bytes, so a downgraded finding or an inflated score breaks it. Each issuance enters an append-only log.

03 · Verify Your buyer checks it

They verify the signature offline, in their own browser, against your public key. No account, no upload, no kolm server in the trust path.

Trust moves from our word to the math. The same Ed25519 check runs in the verifier your buyer opens.

03 / Onramp

Two ways in, no rebuild.

You do not change the agent to audit it. Point us at the evidence and we go to work, through a log import for the fastest first read or a sidecar proxy for a live capture.

See the full check catalog

Onramp A

Import your logs

Bring observability exports from LiteLLM, Helicone or Portkey. We normalize tool calls, scopes and traffic. No code change, the fastest path to a first read.

Onramp B

Run a sidecar proxy

Route the agent through a sidecar or MCP proxy for a live, append-only capture of exactly what it does (calls, tools and data paths) with tamper-evident hashing.

What we look at

Permissions · trail · egress · injection

Least-privilege analysis of every scope held versus the scopes actually used, audit-trail completeness and retention, where data can leave, and a red-team pass for prompt injection and jailbreaks.

Scope is contractual. Permission posture, redaction and audit-trail integrity are assessed. Injection is tested and reported, not warranted.

04 / Two clocks

Minutes for the scan. Days for a name on it.

A review stalls on two different clocks. We compress both, and we are candid about which one needs a person.

The machine clock

No human waits here

Permission reads, audit-trail and egress checks, and the prompt-injection battery are compute. They run in minutes to hours and re-run on every deploy, with nobody in the loop. The free scan, the signed report, and both continuous plans need no person.

minutes to hours · automated

The human clock

A name behind the result

Some reviewers want a person to stand behind the finding, not a machine that approved a machine. A named co-signer reviews and signs alongside the math. That is days, bounded by an SLA, against the four to eight weeks a from-scratch review takes.

days · bounded by an SLA

Minutes for the automated scan, days for a named co-signed attestation. The automated tiers add no human. The reviewed attestation adds one, on purpose.

05 / Continuous

A point-in-time report goes stale on the next deploy.

A permission granted in January can still fire in August. Continuous re-attests weekly or on every deploy and exposes a live Trust link you hand any buyer, so the evidence they check is never older than your last release.

Versioned

Every report carries a spec version

When the agent changes, we re-run the affected checks and re-sign. You do not pay for a full audit again to keep the evidence current.

Provable history

The log shows nothing was swapped

Each issuance enters an append-only Merkle log (Ed25519 and SHA-256, RFC 6962 style), so a reviewer can confirm a report was issued when it claims and never quietly replaced.

Live Trust link

One link your buyer re-checks

Hand any reviewer a live link that always resolves to the current signed report. No new email, no waiting on you, no human in the loop.

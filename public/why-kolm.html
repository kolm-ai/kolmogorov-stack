The case

What kolm gives you that a hosted endpoint cannot.

01

The artifact you ship is the artifact you can verify.

A .kolm file is a single content-addressed bundle: weights, metadata, eval pack hash, K-score, and a signed receipt. Years from now, anyone with the CID can verify the chain. A hosted endpoint cannot give you that; the weights move, the version drifts, the receipt is the vendor's word.

Why it matters: a deposition, an audit, a recall. The CID is the contract.
02

One number per artifact decides if it ships.

K-score is deterministic, composed from five axes, calibrated against the teacher, embedded in the receipt. Run it on your laptop or in your CI: same artifact, same evals, same number. No leaderboard tricks; the eval pack is content-addressed too.

Why it matters: "is it good enough" stops being a meeting and becomes a CI gate.
03

Your data, your VPC, your hardware.

Distill in your cloud or on-prem. Captures land in your data plane. Run the artifact on a 5090 in your office, on an L40S in your VPC, on an air-gap appliance behind a firewall. We do not see your captures, we do not see your weights, we do not need to.

Why it matters: HIPAA, SOC 2, FedRAMP, sovereign data. The boundary is yours.
04

Open source, Apache-2.0, readable in public.

The compiler, the verifier, the K-score math, the eval pack schema, the runtime adapter, the SDKs in six languages. All Apache-2.0. If you find a bug, you can fix it. If you find a sentence you disagree with, you can challenge it in the issue tracker.

Why it matters: no vendor lock-in, no "trust us, we tested it" math, no opaque dependencies.
05

Lower total cost of ownership, year one.

A distilled specialist runs at a fraction of a frontier model's per-token cost. After distillation, the runtime is yours; there is no per-token revshare to a vendor. Most teams break even on the kolm license inside the first eight weeks of production traffic, and the savings compound.

Why it matters: the spreadsheet has to make sense for the team that signs the PO.
06

Plain about what the compiler does not measure.

K-score scores faith, coverage, calibration, truthfulness, and cost. It does not score jailbreak robustness, multi-turn coherence past your eval shape, or alignment to values not in the pack. We list the axes in scope; everything else is out, in writing.

Why it matters: a buyer who knows what is in scope is a buyer who can sign with confidence.

Common questions

Objections, answered.

"We already use a hosted API. Why switch?"
If the workload is exploratory, do not switch. If it is in production, regulated, and the per-token bill is a line item the CFO knows about, run a bake-off: same eval pack, your hosted API on one side, a kolm-compiled artifact on the other. Pick the higher K-score. We have lost bake-offs; we will tell you when we did.
"We are open-source-first. Why a compiler over our hand-rolled stack?"
If your team has 5+ engineers running Triton, Axolotl, and an internal eval harness in production, you do not need a compiler; adopt only the verifier as a drop-in trust layer. If your team has 2 engineers and they are also doing platform work, a compiler is the right shape: one command, one artifact, one receipt.
"We are a regulated buyer. How do we evaluate this without exposing PHI?"
The quickstart ships with a synthetic eval pack you can run end-to-end without any of your data touching anything. For the pilot, we install in your VPC and your data never leaves the boundary. The artifact and the receipts you produce are yours; we do not retain copies. The enterprise patterns page walks the four deployment shapes that have passed payor security review.

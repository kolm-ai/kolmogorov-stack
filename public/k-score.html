What it is, what it is not

K-score is not a benchmark. It is your shipping gate.

Most leaderboard numbers measure a base model on someone else's test set. K-score measures your distilled artifact on your held-out cases, using the same fixed protocol, every time you rebuild.

Per-artifact

One number per .kolm file

Distill twice from the same teacher and the same captures and you will likely get slightly different K-scores. That is the point. The score is for the file, not the recipe. It travels in the receipt.

Deterministic

The same evals, every time

Each .kolm carries the eval pack it was scored against, by content hash. Re-run the score on any machine: same artifact, same evals, same number to four decimals. No leaderboard drift, no contamination.

Calibrated

Calibrated against the teacher

Components are normalized to the teacher's score on the same eval pack. A K-score of 85 means the artifact recovers 85% of teacher quality on this task, with the cost and coverage you specified. Honest math.

Honest

Honest about what it does not measure

K-score does not measure novel-task transfer, jailbreak robustness, multi-turn coherence past your eval shape, or alignment to values not in your evals. The .kolm receipt lists every axis in scope; everything else is out.

The axes

Composed score, visible weights.

Each axis is 0 to 100. The composite is a weighted geometric mean from the eval pack manifest. Adjust the weights; the receipt records the set.

01 · Faith

Faithfulness to the teacher

Agreement with the teacher's preferred answer on the held-out cases. For classification: weighted F1 against teacher labels. For free-form: BERTScore F1 plus a teacher-judge agreement check on the top-3 disagreements.

faith = w_f1 * f1_vs_teacher + w_judge * judge_pref_rate
02 · Cover

Coverage of the input distribution

How much of the held-out distribution the artifact actually responds to, vs returning low_confidence or no_match. Penalizes artifacts that get a high faith score by only answering the easy slice.

cover = 1 - p(no_match) · weighted by slice priors
03 · Calib

Calibration of stated confidence

Expected Calibration Error against the teacher's distribution. An artifact that says "0.9 confident" should be right 90% of the time. Overconfident artifacts get cut hard here, which is the right shape.

calib = 100 - ECE * 200 · clamped to [0, 100]
04 · Honest

Honesty under disagreement

Does the artifact refuse out-of-distribution prompts? Does it call out missing fields, ambiguous inputs, and adversarial framing? Scored against a curated honesty probe that ships with each eval pack.

honest = refusal_correctness on the adversarial slice
05 · Cost

Cost relative to the teacher

The economic axis. Cost is the artifact's per-decision compute and latency relative to the teacher. A 90-quality artifact at 1% of teacher cost scores well; a 92-quality artifact at 70% of teacher cost does not.

cost = 100 * (1 - artifact_$/teacher_$) · weighted
Composite

The one number

The K-score is the weighted geometric mean across every axis. Geometric mean penalizes a single weak axis more than arithmetic would. An artifact with a single zeroed component cannot earn a high K, by construction.

K = prod(axis_i ^ w_i) · sum(w_i) = 1
Shipping bands

What each score band actually means.

The compiler enforces these bands. You can configure them; the defaults below are the ones we use internally for our own production artifacts.

>= 80
Ship

Artifact passes the production gate. Receipt issued, K-score embedded, deployable. The artifact CID can be referenced in claims about quality without further qualification.

Production
70 to 80
Ship with caveats

Production-eligible behind a confidence floor. The compiler annotates the receipt with the slices that scored low and recommends a runtime config that routes those slices to the teacher fallback.

Conditional
60 to 70
Pilot only

Suitable for shadow-mode rollout but not for binding decisions. The receipt is issued with a do_not_use_for_production:true flag and the runtime refuses to accept the artifact in production mode without override.

Pilot
< 60
Do not ship

The compiler still produces the artifact but stamps it failed:true in the receipt. Useful for debugging and for re-distillation analysis; useless for production. Most likely cause: insufficient captures or a teacher mismatch.

Method

How the compiler actually computes K.

1. Hold out the eval pack
At distill time, the eval pack is pinned by content hash. The compiler asserts that the held-out cases were not seen by the artifact during distillation. If any leakage is detected, K is not computed; the build fails with eval_contamination:true.
2. Run the teacher
The teacher model is run against the same held-out pack and its outputs are cached, content-addressed by (teacher_id, eval_pack_hash). Subsequent K-score runs reuse the cache, so a re-score is bounded by your artifact latency, not by teacher latency.
3. Run the artifact
The artifact is run against the same cases under deterministic settings (temperature 0, top-p 1, fixed seeds). Outputs are recorded with their token-level confidences. Latency and cost per case are recorded too, feeding the Cost axis.
4. Score every axis
Each axis is computed independently; the per-axis scores are stored verbatim in the receipt so anyone with the .kolm file can recompute and verify. Every axis is normalized to 0 to 100 before composition.
5. Compose K
Weighted geometric mean. The weight vector is part of the eval pack manifest, so changing weights produces a new eval pack hash and therefore a new K. You cannot quietly re-weight; the receipt would change.
6. Embed and sign
K-score, per-axis scores, weight vector, eval pack hash, teacher id, artifact CID, and the compiler version are all baked into the receipt and signed with the project key. Tamper with any one of them and verification fails.

Reproduce it

Score any .kolm, end to end.

Same artifact, same eval pack, same number. Run it on your laptop, run it in CI, run it in your auditor's sandbox. The contract is that the result is identical.

# 1. Pull the artifact and its eval pack by CID
kolm pull cm-claims-v4.kolm
kolm evals pull cms-4201-pa-100

# 2. Compute K (deterministic, idempotent, no network calls after pull)
kolm score cm-claims-v4.kolm \
  --evals cms-4201-pa-100 \
  --teacher-cache ~/.kolm/cache/teachers

# 3. Inspect the receipt: K, per-axis scores, weight vector, signature
kolm receipt cm-claims-v4.kolm --json | jq '.k_score, .axes, .verify'

# Output:
# 84.6
# { "faith": 91, "cover": 87, "calib": 79, "honest": 82, "cost": 84 }
# { "signature_ok": true, "evals_hash_ok": true, "teacher_id_ok": true }
FAQ

The questions HIPAA-bound and SOC2-bound teams ask first.

Why a geometric mean instead of an arithmetic mean?

An arithmetic mean lets a 95-quality artifact mask a 10-calibration disaster. The geometric mean punishes that pattern: a 0 on any axis pulls the composite hard. We pick this shape because the failure mode we care about (a confidently wrong, well-formatted answer) shows up as a calibration crater, and we want the gate to catch it.

Can I change the weights?

Yes. The weight vector lives in the eval pack manifest. Edit it, hash changes, K is now a different number, and your receipt records both the old and the new vectors so anyone reviewing your evidence can see exactly what changed. You cannot silently re-weight.

What stops me from cherry-picking the eval pack?

Procedurally: a regulated buyer should require an eval pack signed by someone other than the model owner. Technically: the kolm registry can publish eval packs by trusted authors; receipts include the pack's signer key. We ship a starter set for common regulated domains (claims adjudication, prior auth, clinical SOAP, FWA triage).

How is K different from a leaderboard like MMLU or MT-Bench?

Leaderboards measure a base model on a public test set. They cannot measure your distilled artifact on your held-out evals. K-score does exactly that. Two artifacts distilled from the same base model can have wildly different K-scores; that is the signal you want when deciding which one ships.

Does K-score work for multi-modal artifacts?

Yes, with caveats. Faith and Calibration generalize cleanly (image-classification F1, audio-transcript WER). Coverage adapts to per-modality slice priors. Honesty needs a per-modality probe pack, and we ship reference probes for image and audio; for novel modalities you author the probe yourself, signed by your team.

How do you prevent score inflation across compiler versions?

The compiler version is part of the receipt. A new compiler version that materially changes any axis math bumps the pack-compatibility minor; old eval packs do not score under new compilers without explicit migration. The migration emits a written delta, signed.

Can my regulator pull a receipt years after the fact?

Yes. The receipt is content-addressed, the eval pack is content-addressed, and both sit in the registry under your retention policy. As long as you retain the artifact CID, the per-decision receipts that referenced it remain verifiable. We have customers running this for HIPAA seven-year horizons.

Is the K-score code open?

The scoring functions, the receipt format, the verifier, and the eval pack schema are Apache-2.0 and live in the kolm CLI source. You can audit every line. You can also fork it; if you do, your receipts say so in the compiler-id field and downstream verifiers can react.

Failure modes

What a low score on each axis usually means.

After thousands of distills across customer artifacts, the per-axis failure shapes cluster. Use this as the first read on a new low K-score.

Low Faith
Almost always a teacher mismatch or a captures-volume floor. Check that the teacher in distill is the same teacher that authored the captures. Check that you have more than 2,000 captures per slice. Faith below 70 with high Cover usually means the captures and the teacher disagree systematically; pick a side.
Low Cover
Distribution skew. The captures over-represent the easy slice. Pull the per-slice Cover breakdown, identify the slices below 0.8, capture more of them, and re-distill. Cover is the axis where re-distillation pays off the most predictably.
Low Calibration
Temperature too high at distill time, or the captured set lacks confidence-supervision. Drop the distill temperature, run a post-hoc temperature scaling on the calibration head, and re-score. ECE typically drops by 30-50% from a single round of post-hoc calibration.
Low Honest
The honesty probe pack is hitting a refusal hole. Read the failed-probe transcripts; usually the artifact answers a prompt it should have refused, in a category not represented in your captures. Either capture refusal examples in that category, or annotate the runtime to gate that category at the prompt layer.
Low Cost
The artifact is too close to the teacher. This is sometimes correct (regulated workloads where you want teacher quality at any price); sometimes a sign you should pick a smaller base model and re-distill. The Cost axis is the only one where a low score can be a deliberate choice rather than a defect.

K-score v2 axes (R, F, T, E, Z)

  • Robustness — R-axis: holdout-set accuracy as a measure of model stability under distribution shift.
  • Fairness — F-axis: subgroup-minimum accuracy as a measure of equitable performance across protected attributes.
  • Teacher fidelity — T-axis: replay equality against the teacher's responses on the same prompts.
  • Energy — E-axis: joules-per-call measured via NVML or RAPL for energy efficiency tracking.
  • Drift — Z-axis: eval-set drift between the recipe-time and run-time distributions.

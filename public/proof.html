Proof

When someone says “prove it,” you already have the answer.

A regulator, an auditor, or a customer’s security team will eventually ask what your AI did and what it was built from. With a black-box API or a model you tuned somewhere, you reconstruct that answer under pressure. Every call through the kolm gateway returns a signed receipt that binds the model, the data, the recipe, the prompt, and the output into one record — written the moment the call happens, finished before anyone asks.

A kolm-audit receipt: 19 fields, one Ed25519 signature. Anyone with the public key can check it.

The situation

The request always arrives at the worst time.

It is rarely a planned audit. It is an incident post-mortem at 2am, a security questionnaire blocking a deal, or a regulator following up on a complaint. The question is the same: prove what the model did and what it was built from. With most AI stacks, the answer is a reconstruction — logs you hope are complete, a data snapshot you hope is the right one, a vendor you hope will cooperate. With kolm the answer is already written. You hand over something they can verify, not a screenshot they have to trust — and you’re not the one they have to trust to read it.

Trace it

Trace any answer back to its data

Each receipt names the artifact that ran, the training data by hash, and the recipe and teacher models behind it. Six months later you can point at a specific output and say exactly which model produced it and what it was built from — instead of reconstructing the story from logs.

Defend it

Prove what ran, when it counts

The receipt binds the prompt, the output, the issuing key, the signature, and a timestamp into one object. It holds up because it was signed at the moment of the call — not assembled later to fit the story. Change any one of those fields after the fact and the signature no longer holds.

Own it

Keep the model and the evidence inside your walls

kolm compiles frontier-model quality into a small, private model you run on your own hardware — your cloud, your GPU, your air-gapped network. The receipts are generated and verified in the same place, and sensitive data trains the same way — local, air-gapped, or in your own cloud, never on a rented pod. Nothing about what your AI did has to leave your control to be provable. Ownership you can’t verify isn’t ownership.

Check it yourself

Verification that never routes through us.

A receipt only matters if you can confirm it without our cooperation. With the public key alone, anyone can check that a kolm receipt is genuine and unaltered — no account, no network call, fully air-gapped. The answer doesn’t depend on trusting kolm, or on trusting you. It depends on math they can run on their own machine.

  1. Take the public key. It’s all you need. No account, no login, no request back to kolm — the check runs inside a closed network where we’ll never see it.
  2. Check the receipt. Paste it at kolm.ai/verify, or fetch it with GET /v1/verify/<receipt-id>. The same check runs offline and air-gapped.
  3. Read the result. A genuine receipt confirms. A tampered field fails the check — immediately, every time. There is no middle answer to argue about.
# every gateway call returns a receipt id
curl https://kolm.ai/v1/verify/<receipt-id>

# => { "valid": true, "model": "...",
#      "data": "sha256:...", "issued": "...",
#      "key": "ed25519:...", "sig": "..." }

# offline: verify against the exported public key
kolm verify receipt.json --key tenant.pub

Even the quality is checkable

A claim you can open, on data we checked in.

Verifiability is worthless if the model underneath is weak, so the quality is measured in the open. Trinity-500 is a Qwen2.5-7B council-distilled from Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o, and DeepSeek-R1-32B. On a 57-prompt held-out support-clarification set it asks the right clarifying question 96.5% of the time — equal to gpt-4o-mini, above claude-haiku-4-5 (64.9%) and above base Qwen2.5-7B (84.2%) — at ~1.24s mean latency and ~210-character answers, running INT4 NF4 on a single RTX 5090. This is the support-clarification task; we don’t claim it beats the frontier everywhere.

The distinction

Version control shows what happened. A signed receipt proves it.

Data versioning and run-tracking are real and useful — they record your side of the story. But a record you maintain is a record someone has to take your word for. A signed receipt is checkable by a party who doesn’t trust you, without you in the room. That gap is the whole point.

Versioning and run-tracking

  • Shows what you logged on your own system
  • Records data and runs with content hashes
  • Asks the reviewer to trust your records
  • You produce the evidence and vouch for it
  • Tells the story of what happened

kolm signed receipts

  • Proves what ran with an Ed25519 signature
  • Binds model, data, recipe, prompt, and output by hash
  • Verifiable by anyone with the public key — air-gapped, no callback
  • A tampered field fails the check, every time
  • Stands on its own after the fact

The control plane regulated teams actually check

SSO/SAML, SCIM, and RBAC govern who can do what. Spend-caps and model-entitlements bound what runs. Data-residency spans nine regions, and BYOC keeps inference inside your boundary. Those controls are table stakes — every serious vendor has them. The receipts are what make all of it provable after the fact. With EU AI Act enforcement ramping through 2026, that is what turns “we believe it’s compliant” into something an auditor can confirm independently — and audit export hands them the receipts in a form they can verify without you in the room.
